# Thinking

This document captures how I think about product management.

Not as theory.  
As operating principles.

I use these ideas to frame problems, make decisions, design experiments, and build products that create real user and business value.

---

## 1. Start with the user behavior, not the feature

Features do not matter on their own.

What matters is whether the product creates a better user behavior.

When I think about a product, I ask:

- What behavior are we trying to encourage?
- What behavior are we trying to reduce?
- Why is the current behavior happening?
- What would “better” look like in the user’s real workflow?

This helps me stay focused on outcomes instead of output.

---

## 2. Every metric is a story about behavior

Metrics are not just numbers.

They are compressed signals of human behavior.

If activation drops, something became confusing.
If engagement rises but retention falls, something may be attracting the wrong behavior.
If conversion improves but satisfaction declines, the product may be extracting value instead of creating it.

I try to interpret metrics as signals of what users are experiencing, valuing, or struggling with.

---

## 3. Product work is often about resolving tension

Many important product decisions are not about maximizing one thing.

They are about managing tension between two good goals.

Examples:

- growth vs quality
- speed vs trust
- flexibility vs simplicity
- short-term lift vs long-term retention

Strong product judgment comes from naming the tension clearly, understanding the tradeoff, and choosing intentionally.

---

## 4. Debugging starts with truth, not opinions

When something moves in the product, I do not start by jumping to solutions.

I start by grounding in truth.

I ask:

- What exactly changed?
- For whom did it change?
- When did it change?
- Is this real, or is it a data issue?
- What changed in the product, market, or system around that time?

Good debugging is disciplined curiosity.

The goal is not to sound smart early.  
The goal is to get to the truth fast.

---

## 5. Segmentation is where insight begins

Averages hide reality.

When I look at product performance, I want to know:

- new vs returning users
- power vs casual users
- mobile vs desktop
- geography
- acquisition source
- persona or job to be done
- first-time behavior vs repeated behavior

A product problem often becomes clear only when the data is broken into the right segments.

---

## 6. A product should earn the next action

A good product experience creates momentum.

After each user action, the product should make the next step feel obvious, valuable, and easy.

I think a lot about:

- reducing hesitation
- preserving context
- lowering cognitive load
- making progress visible
- making the reward feel immediate

The best products do not just function well.  
They pull the user forward.

---

## 7. Retention is the deepest test of product value

Growth can be bought.
Clicks can be manipulated.
Retention is harder to fake.

If users come back, it usually means one of three things is true:

- the product solves a real recurring problem
- the product fits naturally into a workflow or habit
- the product gets better with repeated use

That is why I take retention seriously.

It forces a higher standard:
not “Did they try it?”
but
“Did it become valuable enough to keep?”

---

## 8. Good strategy starts with choosing what not to do

Strategy is not a list of ambitions.

Strategy is choice.

That means deciding:

- which users matter most right now
- which problem is worth solving first
- which opportunities are distractions
- which tradeoffs we are willing to accept

I respect focus.

In product, lack of focus is often just ambition without discipline.

---

## 9. Simplicity is hard, but it is usually worth it

Complexity often enters products for understandable reasons:
more customers, more edge cases, more requests, more internal stakeholders.

But complexity has a cost:

- slower decisions
- harder onboarding
- lower trust
- more support burden
- weaker adoption

I believe simple products often require more thinking, not less.

Simplicity is not shallow.
It is refined.

---

## 10. Product managers should build systems, not just plans

A PM should not only define what to build.

A PM should improve how decisions get made.

That means building repeatable systems for:

- learning from customers
- generating hypotheses
- prioritizing tradeoffs
- evaluating results
- sharing context
- improving team decision quality

This is one reason I am interested in AI.

AI can help product teams move from one-off thinking to repeatable thinking systems.

---

## 11. AI is most powerful when it improves judgment leverage

I do not think the highest value of AI is just speed.

The deeper value is leverage.

Used well, AI can help product teams:

- explore more options
- pressure-test reasoning
- structure ambiguity
- accelerate synthesis
- turn scattered inputs into usable decisions

That is the area I am most interested in:
using AI to improve the quality and velocity of product thinking.

---

## 12. I value clarity over performance

I try to communicate in a way that makes the problem, decision, and reasoning unmistakably clear.

Not just because clarity sounds better.

Because clarity helps teams align faster, debate the right things, and move with confidence.

I want my communication to do three things:

- level set quickly
- sharpen the decision
- make the next step obvious

---

## 13. Great PMs reduce ambiguity for everyone around them

A strong PM helps the team answer:

- What problem are we solving?
- Why does it matter?
- What does success look like?
- What are the risks?
- What decision do we need to make now?

That is part of how I think about leadership.

Not as having all the answers.
But as creating enough clarity that the team can move.

---

## 14. I respect evidence, but I also respect taste

Not every good product decision comes from a dashboard.

Sometimes it comes from:

- noticing friction others normalize
- seeing a simpler way
- understanding emotional resonance
- recognizing when the product feels heavy, slow, or unclear

Data matters.
But so do taste, pattern recognition, and proximity to the user experience.

The best product work uses both.

---

## 15. What I am building toward

I am especially interested in the future of product management in an AI-native world.

I believe the next generation of strong PMs will not just write PRDs and review dashboards.

They will build:

- decision systems
- experiment systems
- workflow systems
- AI-assisted product loops

That is the direction I am exploring through the work in this GitHub.

---

## Your one takeaway about me in one sentence

I believe product management is the discipline of understanding human behavior, making good decisions under uncertainty, and building systems that help teams create value repeatedly.
